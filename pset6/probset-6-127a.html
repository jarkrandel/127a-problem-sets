    Problems Problems

    1.

    Abbott Exercise 3.2.11(a). Prove that \(\overline{A\cup B} = \overline{A}\cup\overline{B}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    It suffices to prove that \(L_{A\cup B} = L_A\cup L_B\text{,}\) where \(L_C\) is the set of limit points of \(C\) for any \(C\subseteq\R\text{.}\) The inclusion of the right-hand side in the left should be clear from the definition. For the other direction, notice that if \(x\in L_{A\cup B}\) has points of \(A\cup B\) arbitrarily close, it must have points of either \(A\) or \(B\) arbitrarily close.
    🔗

    3.

    1. Prove that if \((a_n)\to a\) and \((b_n)\to b\text{,}\) then \((|a_n-b_n|)\to |a-b|\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use part (a) to help you complete Abbott Exercise 3.3.8(a): let \(K\) and \(L\) be nonempty compact sets, and define \(d = \inf\{|x-y| : x\in K \text{ and } y\in L\}\text{.}\) If \(K\) and \(L\) are disjoint, show \(d > 0\) and that \(d = |x_0-y_0|\) for some \(x_0\in K\) and \(y_0\in L\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Hint.
    For part (a), use the right form of the triangle inequality.
    🔗
    For part (b), take a sequence of points converging to the infimum, and use compactness to extract associated convergent subsequences of points in \(K\) and \(L\) whose limits achieve the infimum.
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Let \(K\subseteq\R\) be a finite set. You may assume that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} K = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_N\} \end{equation*}
    where \(x_1 < x_2 < \cdots < x_N\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Prove that \(K\) is compact using the definition directly.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Prove that \(K\) is compact by showing that it is closed and bounded.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Prove that \(K\) is compact by showing that every open subcover of \(K\) has a finite subcover.
      🔗
      🔗
    Hint.
    For part (a), if you have infinitely many items and only finitely many boxes to put them in, then one box must contain infinitely many items.
    🔗
    For part (b), you can use the definition of closed directly or use the characterization of closed sets as complements of open sets. The latter is easier here.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Optional. The following optional exercises give different proofs of the remaining implication in Heine-Borel: closed and bounded implies every open cover has a finite subcover.
    🔗
    Abbott Exercise 3.3.9. Assume \(K\) is closed and bounded and let \(\{O_\lambda : \lambda\in\Lambda\}\) be an open cover for \(K\text{.}\) For contradiction, assume that no finite subcover exists. Let \(I_0\) be a closed interval containing \(K\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Show that there exists a nested sequence of closed intervals \(I_0 \supseteq I_1 \supseteq I_2 \supseteq \cdots\) such that, for each \(n\text{,}\) \(I_n\cap K\) cannot be finitely covered and \(\lim |I_n| = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Argue that there exists an \(x\in K\) such that \(x\in I_n\) for all \(n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Because \(x\in K\text{,}\) there must exist an open set \(O_{\lambda_0}\) from the original collection that contains \(x\text{.}\) Explain how this leads to the desired contradiction.
      🔗
      🔗
    Abbott Exercise 3.3.10. Consider the special case where \(K\) is a closed interval. Let \(\{O_\lambda : \lambda\in\Lambda\}\) be an open cover for \([a,b]\) and define \(S\) to be the set of all \(x\in[a,b]\) such that \([a,x]\) has a finite subcover from \(\{O_\lambda : \lambda\in\Lambda\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Argue that \(S\) is nonempty and bounded, and thus \(s = \sup S\) exists.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(s = b\text{,}\) which implies \([a,b]\) has a finite subcover.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Finally, prove the theorem for an arbitrary closed and bounded set \(K\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
