    Problem 1: Do the following series converge or diverge? Justify your answers using our theorems or the definition directly. You do not have to write out the statements, but remind yourself of the statements of the Divergence Test, Comparison Test, Absolute Convergence Test, Ratio Test, and Alternating Series Test.
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{2n+4}{4n-1}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{n^{1/3}}{n^2 + 10\sqrt{n}}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{n^n}{n!}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Let \(0 {\lt} r {\lt} 1\text{,}\) \(k\in \mathbb {N}\text{,}\) and consider \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty n^kr^n\)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{3^n-n}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution: In all of the following parts, let \(a_n\) denote the \(n\)-th term of the sequence being summed in the series we’re considering.
    🔗
    1. Notice that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_na_n = \lim_n\frac{2+\frac{4}{n}}{4 - \frac{1}{n}} = \frac{1}{2}\not= 0. \end{equation*}
      So, this series diverges by the Divergence Test.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Notice that \(a_n \leq n^{1/3}/n^2 = n^{-5/3}\text{.}\) Since \(5/3 {\gt} 1\text{,}\) the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-5/3}\) converges, and \(\sum_na_n\) converges by the Comparison Test.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Observe that for all \(n\in \mathbb {N}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} a_n = \frac{n^n}{n!} = \frac{n\cdot n \cdot n\cdots n}{1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdots n} \geq 1. \end{equation*}
      Therefore, \(a_n\) does not converge to 0, and the series diverges by the Divergence Test.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Since the sequence \(b_n = 1/\sqrt{n}\) is decreasing and satisfies \(\lim_nb_n = 0\text{,}\) the Alternating Series Test implies \(a_n\) converges.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Let’s compute the ratio \(a_{n+1}/a_n\text{.}\) Notice
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{(n+1)^kr^{n+1}}{n^kr^n} = \left(\frac{n+1}{n}\right)^kr = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^k r. \end{equation*}
      Since \(\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty }(1+\frac{1}{n})^k = (\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty }(1+\frac{1}{n}))^k = 1,\) (by the Algebraic Limit Theorem) we know that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty }\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = r {\lt} 1. \end{equation*}
      Therefore, the Ratio Test implies this series converges.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. This should behave like a geometric series, so let’s use the ratio test. Notice that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| =\frac{3^n-n}{3^{n+1}-n} = \frac{1-n3^{-n}}{3-n3^{-n}}. \end{equation*}
      But, since \((n3^{-n})\rightarrow 0\) (We could conclude this by part (e) since \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty }n3^{-n}\) converges), we know
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty }\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{1 - 0}{3 - 0} = \frac{1}{3} {\lt} 1. \end{equation*}
      So, this series converges by the Ratio Test.
      🔗
      🔗
    Problem 2: It is true that for any two sequences \((a_n)\) and \((b_n)\) that differ at only finitely many terms, one converges if and only if the other converges. Use this fact to prove that for any two such sequences \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges if and only if \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\) converges.
    🔗
    Solution: Let \((s_m)\) denote the sequence of partial sums of \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\text{,}\) and let \((t_m)\) denote the sequence of partial sums for \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\text{.}\) The fact that \((a_n)\) and \((b_n)\) differ at only finitely many indices means that there exists an integer \(N\) such that \(a_n = b_n\) for all \(n\geq N\text{.}\) Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s_m - s_N = \sum_{n=N+1}^ma_n = \sum_{n=N+1}^mb_n = t_m - t_N \end{equation*}
    for all \(m\geq N\text{.}\) This then gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s_m = t_m + (s_N - t_N) \end{equation*}
    where \(s_N - t_N\) is just some constant. Therefore, if \((t_m)\) converges, then \(s_m\) converges by the Algebraic Limit Theorem. On the other hand, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t_m = s_m + (t_N-s_N) \end{equation*}
    so that if \((s_m)\) converges, then \((t_m)\) converges too for the same reason.
    🔗
    Problem 3: Complete Exercise 2.7.8.
    🔗
    Solution:
    🔗
    1. True. The intuition is that absolutely convergent series have very tiny terms for large \(n\text{,}\) and squaring any number \(\leq 1\) makes its magnitude even smaller. Since convergence of series only depends on the “tail end” behavior (just like for sequences), there is a point beyond which \(|a_n|^2 \leq |a_n|\text{,}\) and you can bound the difference of partial sums of the squared series by the original series.
      🔗
      Since \(\sum_na_n\) converges, we know that \(\lim_na_n = 0\text{.}\) We will use the Cauchy Criterion to show that \(\sum_na_n^2\) also converges absolutely. Let \(\epsilon {\gt} 0\) and let \(N\) be an integer such that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=m}^n|a_k|{\lt} \epsilon \text{ and } |a_n| \leq 1 \text{ for any } n,m\geq N \end{equation*}
      We claim that this choice of \(N\) works for showing that \(\sum_n|a_n|^2\) satisfies the Cauchy Criterion. Indeed, for any \(n {\gt} m \geq N\text{,}\) the fact that \(|a_n| \leq 1\) implies \(|a_n|^2 \leq |a_n|\) so that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=m}^n|a_k^2| \leq \sum_{k=m}^n|a_k| {\lt} \epsilon . \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. False. Take
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} a_n = b_n = \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}. \end{equation*}
      The series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}\) converges by the alternating series test, yet \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_nb_n = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n}\text{,}\) which diverges.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. True. The idea is to compare \(a_n\) with \(\frac{1}{n^2}\text{,}\) a series that converges absolutely.
      🔗
      One way is to do this is to prove the contrapositive. Assume that \(\sum_nn^2a_n\) converges. Our goal will be to show that \(a_n\) converges absolutely, not conditionally. Since \(\sum_nn^2a_n\) converges, we know that \(\lim_n n^2a_n = 0\text{.}\) This means that there exists some \(N\in \mathbb {N}\) such that \(|n^2a_n| \leq 1\) for all \(n\geq N\text{.}\) Rearranging this gives \(|a_n| \leq 1/n^2\) for all \(n\geq N\text{.}\) Comparing \(a_n\) with \(\sum_{n=N}^\infty 1/n^2 {\lt} \infty \text{,}\) we see that \(a_n\) converges absolutely.
      🔗
      🔗
    Problem 4: Suppose that \((a_n)\) is a sequence such that the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_{n+1}-a_n|\) converges. Prove that \((a_n)\) is a Cauchy sequence.
    🔗
    Solution: We will use the Cauchy Criterion for series to verify the definition of Cauchy for \((a_n)\) holds. Let \(\epsilon {\gt} 0\text{.}\) The Cauchy criterion for series says that there exists \(N_0\) such that for any \(n\) and \(m\) satisfying \(n {\gt} m \geq N_0\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=m+1}^n|a_{k+1}-a_k| {\lt} \epsilon . \end{equation*}
    We claim that we can take \(N = N_0 + 1\) to verify that \((a_n)\) is Cauchy. Indeed, for any \(n {\gt} m \geq N\text{,}\) we have \(n {\gt} m-1 \geq N-1 = N_0\) so that we can apply the above condition with \(n-1\) and \(m-1\) in place of \(n\) and \(m\) respectively to get
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |a_n - a_m| = |(a_n-a_{n-1}) + (a_{n-1}-a_{n-2}) + \cdots +(a_{m+1}-a_m)| \leq \sum_{k=m}^{n-1}|a_{k+1}-a_k| {\lt} \epsilon . \end{equation*}
